Kinetic Sculpture!

This weeks objective was to make a kinetic sculpture powered by a motor. I'm glad the brief was so vague.

Original Goal:

I wanted to make a somewhat reminiscent Sisyphus during this project and this Lego Tutorial seemed like the perfect place to start.

I was hoping to at least make the mechanism for the movement of the legs. This is probably the most important part (and also looked a lot less complicated)

My much simpler focus:

What I managed to make

I was able to put together the mechanism that controls the legs!! But I was not able to make the actual legs or anything to hold it in place.

Honestly I'm counting it as a win, look at my lil guy

Challenges at every corner

Step 1: The Bar Linkage

I got the dimensions of the bar linkages from the video and then messed around with them on this odd site and then on MotionGen.


After that, I laser cut my pieces out according to this Fusion360 sketch



This was going a little too well and I was getting suspicious. I was right. When I tried to combine the pieces, the screw heads and nuts sticking out on the opposite ends of the screw hole cutout would interfere with itself. Specifically on the bottom left joint shown in the image below. I worked around this by power drilling the cardboard :).

This made a bigger hole and allowed the screw head to be flush with the cardboard and the nut was just sticking out on the non-interfering side. It's hard to see in the moving picture below but take my word for it.

Step 2: The motor

Now that I had a functional linkage working the next step was to cut out pieces that would interface with the motor. Like many I found out that the cardboard does not hold it's ground against the strength of the motor.

To fix the flimsiness, I opted to use clear acrylic and cut out new pieces. There was only one usable sheet so I was conservative with the kerf and ended up with holes too small. Nothing some power drilling couldn't fix though :))



There was also a lot of issues with getting the pieces to move smoothly, often the lock nuts were slipping off because I couldn't tighten them enough. Figuring out the right combination of spacers for the middle piece was especially challenging.

Step 3: fixing it in place Keeping it on a leash

That's the plan I had for the base

Unfortunately I was really tired the robot did not want to be stuck to the ground forced to move nowhere. I did have to leash it with the cables so it didn't run away.

I'd really like to work more with the microcontroller to see if I can stop it from flailing around quite as much. It'll also be necessary for my final project. Creating a gear assembly to gear down the motor would be really helpful too, it really does not need to be that powerful.

The sketch makes more sense if you look at it long enough. The weird stick thing is to support the green body piece while the legs move.
